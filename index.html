Gotham City Times

Thursday July 7th 1777

Gotham's Slowest Hero Saves the Day

Slothman Lives!

Author

Local residents of Briar Hollow were left both baffled and relieved late Tuesday evening when the city's resident vigilante, Slothman, successfully apprehended a jewelry store bandit — roughly four hours after the initial robbery was reported. Witnesses say the caped crusader was spotted dangling from a fire escape on Mulberry Street around dusk, moving with his trademark unhurried resolve toward the scene of the crime. Police on the scene confirmed that the suspect had, in fact, fallen asleep waiting for his getaway driver when Slothman finally arrived. "He gets there," said Officer Darlene Pruitt. "You just have to be patient."
Little is known about the mysterious figure who prowls Briar Hollow's rooftops at a leisurely pace of approximately one city block per hour. He is described as a broad-shouldered man in a grey bodysuit adorned with curved claws and a cowl bearing two small, drowsy-looking ears. His utility belt, according to eyewitnesses, contains an assortment of gadgets including a grappling hook operated at one-quarter speed, a bola launcher, and what appears to be a small travel pillow. Crime analysts note that his mere presence in a neighborhood has a measurable deterrent effect, as most criminals simply lose interest and leave before he can engage them.

Slothman's origin story, as pieced together by the Briar Hollow Gazette from anonymous tips and one very chatty city archivist, suggests a traumatic encounter in a wildlife sanctuary at a young age left him with an unshakeable bond with the world's most deliberate mammal. He returned from years of training in the remote cloud forests of Central America with iron grip strength, uncanny patience, and a deeply unhurried worldview. Villains who have faced him in hand-to-hand combat report that his fighting style is, in their words, "weirdly effective" and "deeply unsettling to watch," owing to its slow, methodical, and utterly unstoppable nature.
City Mayor Constance Aldridge issued a statement Thursday acknowledging Slothman's contributions to public safety while gently noting that "response time remains an area of opportunity." The vigilante has not publicly responded to the mayor's remarks, though a source close to the matter says a formal reply is expected sometime next week, possibly Wednesday, depending on how his Tuesday nap goes. Briar Hollow residents, for their part, seem broadly supportive. "He's not fast," admitted longtime shop owner Gerald Mwangi, "but nothing gets past him. Eventually." The Gazette will continue to follow this story as it develops, at whatever pace Slothman sees fit.
